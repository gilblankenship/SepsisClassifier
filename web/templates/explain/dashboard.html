Model Explainability Dashboard

SHAP-based explanations showing how each biomarker contributes to predictions.

About This Tool

This classifier uses two urinary biomarkers — CRP/Cr (C-Reactive Protein normalized to Creatinine) and IP-10/Cr (Interferon gamma-Induced Protein 10 normalized to Creatinine) — to predict the likelihood of sepsis. Normalizing to creatinine accounts for variation in urine concentration between patients.

CRP is an acute-phase inflammatory protein produced by the liver in response to infection. IP-10 (CXCL10) is a chemokine released by immune cells during the innate immune response. Elevated urinary levels of both markers are associated with systemic infection and sepsis.

The visualizations below use SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations), a method that quantifies how much each biomarker contributes to an individual prediction. This helps clinicians understand why the model classifies a sample as sepsis-positive or sepsis-negative, rather than relying on a “black box” score alone.

How to read this plot

Each dot represents one patient sample. The horizontal position shows the SHAP value — how much that biomarker pushed the prediction toward sepsis (right) or away from sepsis (left). Dot color indicates the actual biomarker concentration (red = high, blue = low). A cluster of red dots pushed to the right means high levels of that biomarker are strongly associated with a sepsis-positive prediction.

How to read this plot

This bar chart ranks each biomarker by its average impact on the model’s predictions. A longer bar means that biomarker has a greater overall influence on the sepsis classification. This helps identify which biomarker is the primary driver of the model’s decisions and whether the model is placing clinically appropriate weight on each marker.

How to read this plot

This 2D map plots CRP/Cr on one axis and IP-10/Cr on the other. The colored regions show the model’s decision zones — samples falling in the red-shaded area are classified as sepsis-positive, while those in the blue-shaded area are classified as sepsis-negative. Individual patient samples are overlaid as dots. Look for how cleanly the two groups separate; overlap between them indicates cases where the model is less certain.

How to read this plot

The ROC (Receiver Operating Characteristic) curve shows the trade-off between sensitivity (correctly identifying sepsis cases) and specificity (correctly identifying non-sepsis cases) at every possible classification threshold. A curve closer to the top-left corner indicates better performance. The AUC (Area Under the Curve) summarizes overall accuracy — an AUC of 1.0 is perfect, 0.5 is no better than chance. Literature benchmarks are included for comparison with published diagnostic methods.

How to read this plot

The calibration curve assesses whether the model’s predicted probabilities match observed outcomes. A perfectly calibrated model follows the diagonal line — for example, among patients given a 70% sepsis probability, approximately 70% should actually have sepsis. Curves above the diagonal indicate the model underestimates risk; curves below indicate overestimation. Good calibration is essential for clinicians to trust the predicted probability when making treatment decisions.
